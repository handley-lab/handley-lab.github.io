By [David Yallup](https://yallup.github.io), Namu Kroupa, and [Will Handley](https://willhandley.co.uk) — published in [TMLR, May 2026](https://openreview.net/forum?id=5mF2eRl3gt). Open-source implementation: [`yallup/nss`](https://github.com/yallup/nss).

## What the paper does

How do you make nested sampling run on a GPU? The standard PolyChord-style slice-sampling step is intrinsically sequential — each likelihood call depends on the last — which leaves modern accelerators idle. We introduce **Nested Slice Sampling (NSS)**: a vectorised reformulation that uses hit-and-run slice updates batched across live points, together with a near-optimal rule for setting the slice width that makes per-step compute predictable enough to compile and parallelise. The result keeps PolyChord-quality evidence estimates while running at GPU throughput, and on hard multimodal targets it can outperform tempered-SMC baselines. Validation spans synthetic mixtures, high-dimensional Bayesian problems, and Gaussian-process hyperparameter marginalisation.

The reference implementation ([`yallup/nss`](https://github.com/yallup/nss)) is open source and designed to be extensible — the constrained-update kernel and the slice-width tuning are exposed as composable pieces so downstream methods can swap them out.

## How AI helped (or didn't)

Most of this work predates the current generation of large models — the methodology, JAX implementation, and experiments were done by hand. Where AI assistance has shown up is at the edges: drafting and tightening the manuscript text, and in the downstream extensions and applications built on top of NSS, where pattern-matching against the existing kernel makes new likelihoods quick to wire in.
