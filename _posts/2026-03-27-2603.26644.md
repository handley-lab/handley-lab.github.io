By Toby Lovick, [David Yallup](https://orcid.org/0000-0003-4716-5817), and [Will Handley](https://willhandley.co.uk).

## What the paper does

Hierarchical Bayesian models in cosmology and beyond often carry hundreds or thousands of latent parameters — one per supernova, per quasar light curve, per source. Standard nested sampling scales roughly as the cube of the parameter dimension, putting the full posterior out of reach when the latent dimension is large. We introduce **Automatic Laplace Collapsed Sampling (ALCS)**: at each nested-sampling likelihood evaluation, the latent variables $z$ are collapsed to a scalar contribution via maximum-a-posteriori optimisation and a Laplace approximation around the mode, both computed by autodiff. The effective sampling dimension drops from $d_\theta + d_z$ to just $d_\theta$, making Bayesian evidence tractable for problems where a full hierarchical sample would otherwise require a decade of GPU time.

Although motivated by cosmological applications, ALCS is a general inference method. The paper validates it against the [inference gym](https://github.com/tensorflow/probability/tree/main/spinoffs/inference_gym) suite of community benchmark targets, demonstrating broad applicability beyond any single scientific domain.

## How AI helped (or didn't)

The core mathematical idea — autodiff through the MAP step, Hessian inversion, and Laplace correction — was human-conceived (David's). The theory was worked out on paper first; AI was not involved in the derivation.

Where AI was genuinely transformative was in applying the method at scale. Once a working implementation existed for one likelihood, the pattern became: give the agent the original likelihood, the ALCS-wrapped version, and a new likelihood, and ask it to apply the same transformation. It is very effective at this — if you give it A and A→B, it can reliably produce C→D for a new C. Applying ALCS across a whole suite of well-defined likelihoods, which would have taken a human a long time, became largely hands-off.

The one area where AI actively pushed back unhelpfully was parallel MAP-finding. JAX compilation of optimisation loops is genuinely difficult (optimisers involve conditionals and control flow that don't JIT cleanly). The agent repeatedly claimed it had found the best achievable performance, when in fact significant gains remained. Getting JAX to run parallel MAP optimisation correctly — specifically, finding the right placement of the JIT boundary within the loop — required sustained human judgement that the agent lacked.

Weekly meetings with Will were recorded and transcribed, then fed into the working directory to generate actionable to-do lists: what was discussed last week, what's been done, what's outstanding. A low-friction way to keep the project moving between sessions.
